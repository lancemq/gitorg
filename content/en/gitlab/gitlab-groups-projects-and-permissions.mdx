# GitLab Groups, Projects, and Permissions

## Why this topic matters early

Teams new to GitLab often focus first on code flow and overlook the organizational model.  
But in GitLab, groups, subgroups, projects, and roles directly affect:

- who can view repositories
- who can push
- who can deploy
- who can change CI/CD variables and protected settings

So the permissions model is not merely admin work. It is part of the collaboration design.

## Start with group versus project

- `Group`: the organizational container
- `Project`: the repository and its immediate collaboration surface

Once subgroups are involved, membership and permissions may cascade downward.

## Why inheritance needs care

Permission inheritance is convenient, but careless structure can create problems quickly:

- external collaborators may see too much
- too many users may gain broad operating power
- CI/CD and protected-branch boundaries can become unclear

## Safer operating habits

1. design group boundaries before scaling members
2. place projects intentionally, not by convenience alone
3. use more conservative access for external contributors and automation identities
4. avoid over-assigning high roles

## Common mistakes

- treating GitLab as only code hosting and ignoring organization design
- giving Maintainer or stronger access too freely
- adding subgroups before understanding the inheritance chain
